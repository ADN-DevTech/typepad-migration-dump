<p>By <a href="http://adndevblog.typepad.com/autocad/stephen-preston.html">Stephen Preston</a></p>  <p><a href="http://adndevblog.typepad.com/autocad/2012/05/vbnet-or-c-a-personal-perspective.html">Yesterday</a>, in a rather roundabout way, I introduced you to my personal investigation (as a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" target="_blank">Java</a> newbie) into how easy it is to convert existing C# code to Java. I’ve already explained that the hardest part for me was polishing up my C# skills (I keep forgetting the semicolons and curly brackets). My plan was to write a relatively (but not too) simple AutoCAD C# plug-in, convert the basic business logic to Java, and then embed that in an <a href="http://developer.android.com/index.html" target="_blank">Android app</a>. I wanted this exercise to be about the C# and the Java. Therefore, I deliberately stuck to a non-graphical application, so I didn’t have to start learning additional graphics libraries. I decided on a simple commandline binary prioritization helper plug-in.</p>  <p>In this post, I’ll show you the C# and give you a working AutoCAD plug-in.</p>  <p>The idea of binary prioritization (or comparison) is to prioritize a list by comparing each item in the list against every other item in the list. The item that you prefer the most after summing all your comparisons is the most important item. Its an obvious tool for a programmer <img style="border-bottom-style: none; border-right-style: none; border-top-style: none; border-left-style: none" class="wlEmoticon wlEmoticon-smile" alt="Smile" src="/assets/image_112058.jpg" />. To demonstrate, here’s a simple example -</p>  <p>I’ve just moved house, and I have along list of tasks I need to do to. Here is a small part of my ToDo list:</p>  <ol>   <li>Fix the roof (before the winter rain comes) </li>    <li>Paint the spare bedroom (because we can’t assemble our bookcases until we’ve painted) </li>    <li>Paint the house exterior (before the winter rain comes) </li>    <li>Dig up the lawn (to plant a vegetable garden) </li>    <li>Build a fence (to stop our chickens – which we haven’t bought yet - escaping) </li> </ol>  <p>In my binary comparison, I record the answers to the following questions:</p>  <blockquote>   <p>Q. Which is more important – ‘fix the roof’ or ‘paint the spare bedroom’?</p>    <p>A. <a href="http://www.wrcc.dri.edu/cgi-bin/cliMAIN.pl?casraf+sfo" target="_blank">The rain probably won’t come until November</a>, but I can’t unpack all my books until the spare bedroom is painted, so painting the room is more important.</p>    <p>Q. Which is more important – ‘fix the roof’ or ‘paint the house exterior’?</p>    <p>A. The painting could probably wait another year at a pinch, so fixing the roof is more important.</p>    <p>Q. Which is more important – ‘fix the roof’ or ‘dig up the lawn’?</p>    <p>A. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_homesteading" target="_blank">Urban homesteading</a> can wait, the rain won’t – fixing the roof is more important.</p>    <p>Etc.</p>    <p>Etc.</p> </blockquote>  <p>I keep doing this until I’ve compared every item with every other item. I then find that I chose each item the following number of times:</p>  <ul>   <li>Paint the spare bedroom : 4 </li>    <li>Fix the roof : 3 </li>    <li>Paint the house exterior : 2 </li>    <li>Build a fence : 1 </li>    <li>Dig up the lawn : 0 </li> </ul>  <p>And that’s my prioritized task list. Off I go to buy some paint brushes and <a href="http://home.howstuffworks.com/home-improvement/construction/materials/low-voc-paint.htm" target="_blank">VOC-free paint</a>.</p>  <p>So now for my C# code.
