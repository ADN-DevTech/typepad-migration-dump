<p>I was chatting with a colleague in Tel Aviv, yesterday, and he gave me the heads-up on a project that was about to be launched on <a href="http://labs.autodesk.com" target="_blank">Autodesk Labs</a>, developed by the Autodesk Research team based in his office (thanks, Benny! :-). I checked this morning and there it was…</p>  <p>The project is called <a href="http://labs.autodesk.com/technologies/shapeshifter" target="_blank">Project Shapeshifter</a> – accessible via <a href="http://shapeshifter.io" target="_blank">shapeshifter.io</a> – and here’s what it says in its About box:</p>  <blockquote>   <p><em>Project Shapeshifter is a free technology preview by Autodesk. It provides an easy way to create complex 3D Printable models in your web browser. With a simple tweak of sliders, you can control the object's shape and select a 3D pattern that wraps around it.</em></p> </blockquote>  <p>It’s really very cool. Here are a few intro videos to give you a sense of what it’s about, one showing <a href="http://youtu.be/jUIK3PzbVfQ" target="_blank">basic</a> and the other showing <a href="http://youtu.be/fEn5OsHCCjQ" target="_blank">advanced</a> controls.</p>  <p>I took it for a quick spin and came up with this model:</p>  <p><a href="http://through-the-interface.typepad.com/.a/6a00d83452464869e2019b00b94340970b-pi" target="_blank"><img style="background-image: none; border-bottom: 0px; border-left: 0px; margin: 20px auto; padding-left: 0px; padding-right: 0px; display: block; float: none; border-top: 0px; border-right: 0px; padding-top: 0px" title="Project ShapeShifter" border="0" alt="Project ShapeShifter" src="/assets/image_998498.jpg" width="470" height="344" /></a></p>  <p>Full disclosure: the “Randomize” button did most of the work for me – I just closed the bottom, added a lip and changed the colour – but it was fun to see how you could use the provided controls to tweak the geometry (which can also be shared, by the way, so <a href="http://shapeshifter.io/#/share/MSAxIDE2IDggMy42MiA5LjQ5IDAuNTUgMC45OSA3NSAwLjIwIDAuNjcgMC42NiAzLjk2IDAgMCAyLjkwIDAgMC41MCAwIDEgMSAxLjgwIDMgIzc5ZmZhOCAyMA==" target="_blank">here it is</a>). The technology reminds me of a simple – but currently more visually dynamic – version of DesignScript. Very handy for quickly creating complex 3D printable models, just as it says in the box. :-)</p>  <p>The other 3D printing-related tech preview that I’ve so far missed talking about is <a href="http://labs.autodesk.com/utilities/miller" target="_blank">Project Miller</a>, which also has a few <a href="http://youtu.be/rUSW33h69X0" target="_blank">intro</a> <a href="http://youtu.be/dIpd0UDSpUc" target="_blank">videos</a> <a href="http://youtu.be/pWIVdoK2vq4" target="_blank">available</a>. This is a tool that’s about preparing 3D models for printing: you can simulate the whole 3D printing process and fix issues in advance, saving you both time and filament.</p>  <p>I went ahead and downloaded Project Miller and installed it on both OS X and Windows. The main difference between the two platforms seems to be that the Windows version includes RealDWG and the FBX SDK and can import from .DWG, .DXF and .FBX.</p>  <p>I found that I could load the .OBJ file created in Project Shapeshifter into Project Miller:</p>  <p><a href="http://through-the-interface.typepad.com/.a/6a00d83452464869e2019b00b9128a970c-pi" target="_blank"><img style="background-image: none;
