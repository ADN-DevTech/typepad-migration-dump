<p>I was happy to see <a href="http://labs.blogs.com/its_alive_in_the_lab/2014/03/project-carbon-now-available-on-autodesk-labs.html" target="_blank">Scott’s announcement</a> that a tool I’d seen demoed back at AU – and during <a href="http://through-the-interface.typepad.com/through_the_interface/2014/01/leaving-israel.html" target="_blank">my recent trip to Israel</a> – has now been <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/US/app/id834082001?mt=8" target="_blank">published to the iOS App Store</a> (even if it’s really a Labs release, at this stage). It was developed by the Applied Research group in our Tel Aviv office, the team that brought you <a href="http://shapeshifter.io" target="_blank">ShapeShifter</a>.</p>  <p>It’s an iPad app called <em>Vectorize It</em> that allows you to generate vectors from raster images – whether taken by the iPad’s camera or picked from its image gallery – with the capability of opening the resultant vector drawing directly in AutoCAD 360. </p>  <p>The app uses <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_detection" target="_blank">edge detection</a> – a standard <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_vision" target="_blank">computer vision</a> technique – to extract vectors from the raster input. It also uses a currently internal mechanism – which will hopefully be useable externally, too, at some point – that transfers geometry definitions to AutoCAD 360 using a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JSON" target="_blank">JSON</a>-based format. Cool stuff.</p>  <p>Let’s take it for a quick spin…</p>  <p>To start with, you get the choice to take a photo, choose an image or use a provided sample.</p>  <p><a href="http://through-the-interface.typepad.com/.a/6a00d83452464869e201a73d9884ba970d-pi" target="_blank"><img style="background-image: none;
